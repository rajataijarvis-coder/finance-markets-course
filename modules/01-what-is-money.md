# Module 1: What is Money?

**Learning time:** 15-20 minutes  
**Prerequisites:** None — this is where we start!

---

## 🤔 The Big Question

Before we understand markets, we need to understand **money** — because markets are just places where money flows between people who want different things.

**Money is not just currency notes.** It's something far more interesting.

---

## 📖 The Story of Money

### Step 1: Barter (The Problem)

Imagine a world without money:
- You have chickens and want shoes
- The shoemaker wants bread, not chickens
- The baker wants apples, not chickens
- The farmer wants shoes...

**The problem:** "Double coincidence of wants" — you need to find someone who has what you want AND wants what you have.

This is inefficient. Very inefficient.

### Step 2: Commodity Money (The Solution)

People started using things everyone wanted as "intermediate" goods:
- Salt (hence "salary" — from Latin *salarium*)
- Shells
- Cattle (hence "capital")
- Gold and silver

**Why gold?**
- Rare (but not too rare)
- Doesn't rust or degrade
- Easy to divide
- Universally valued
- Portable

### Step 3: Representative Money

Carrying gold is risky and heavy. So banks started issuing **receipts** for gold stored in vaults.

These receipts became **paper money** — promises to pay gold.

> 💡 **Key insight:** Paper money started as an IOU for something real (gold).

### Step 4: Fiat Money (Today)

In 1971, the US stopped backing dollars with gold. Now money is:
- **Fiat** = "Let it be done" (by government decree)
- Valued because the government says so
- Valued because we all agree it has value

> 🧠 **Mind-bender:** Modern money has value because we believe it does.

---

## 🏛️ The Three Functions of Money

### 1. Medium of Exchange
- You can trade money for anything, anywhere in the world
- Eliminates barter problems across borders
- **Examples:**
  - UK: Pay £50 for groceries at Tesco
  - US: Pay $100 for dinner in New York  
  - Europe: Pay €80 for train tickets in Paris

### 2. Store of Value
- Money keeps its worth (mostly) over time
- You can save today and spend tomorrow
- **Examples:**
  - UK: Put £1,000 in a savings account at Barclays
  - US: Put $5,000 in a Chase savings account
  - Europe: Put €2,000 in a Deutsche Bank account

### 3. Unit of Account
- Common measure of value across global markets
- Makes comparison shopping possible internationally
- **Examples:**
  - UK: "That BMW costs £45,000"
  - US: "That Tesla costs $55,000"
  - Europe: "That Mercedes costs €50,000"

| Type | Example | What It Is |

## 🏦 Where Money Comes From (Simplified)

### Central Banks Create "Base Money"
- **UK:** Bank of England (BoE) issues pounds (£)
- **US:** Federal Reserve (Fed) issues dollars ($)
- **Europe:** European Central Bank (ECB) issues euros (€)
- **India:** Reserve Bank of India (RBI) issues rupees (₹)

### Commercial Banks Create "Broad Money"
- When any bank lends, they create new money
- **Example:** You deposit £1,000 at HSBC → HSBC lends £900 → Total money = £1,900
- This is called **fractional reserve banking** and happens globally

> 🧮 **Fun fact:** Most money in the world exists only as accounting entries, not physical cash.

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Money solves the barter problem** — no need for "double coincidence of wants"

2. **Money has three functions** — medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account

3. **Modern money is fiat** — valued by trust and government decree, not gold

4. **Most money is digital globally** — only ~3-8% exists as physical cash

5. **Banks worldwide create money through lending** — every loan creates new deposits
