## 🤔 The Big Picture

When a company needs money to grow, it has two choices:
1. **Borrow** — Take a loan, pay interest
2. **Sell ownership** — Issue shares, give investors a stake

**Equity markets** are where the second option happens. When you buy a stock, you become a partial owner of a company.

---

## 🏢 What is Equity?

**Equity** = ownership stake in a company

When you buy shares, you own a tiny piece of that business. Your ownership percentage = (your shares ÷ total shares) × 100

### Why Companies Issue Stock

| Reason | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Raise capital for growth | Tesla raising money to build factories |
| Fund acquisitions | Unilever buying a new brand |
| Refinance debt | BP issuing shares to pay off bonds |
| Reward employees | Options grants to engineers at SAP |

### Why Investors Buy Stock

| Reason | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Capital gains** | Buy BP at £10, sell at £15 → profit |
| **Dividends** | Shell pays ~4% dividend annually |
| **Ownership** | Vote on company decisions |
| **Diversification** | Own pieces of many companies |

## 📊 How Stock Prices Work

### Supply & Demand

Stock prices are simply what buyers are willing to pay and sellers will accept:

```
                Price
                   ↑
    High demand   │           /\
    (buyers)      │          /  \  ← Price rises
                   │         /    \
                   │        /      \
    High supply   │       /        \___
    (sellers)     │      /              \  ← Price falls
                   │     /                \
                   └─────────────────────────→
                        Quantity traded
```

### Market Capitalization

**Market cap** = Share price × Total shares

```
Example: Apple (AAPL)
- Share price: ~$180
- Shares outstanding: ~15.4 billion
- Market cap: $180 × 15.4B = ~$2.8 trillion

Example: BP plc
- Share price: ~£4.50
- Shares outstanding: ~3.3 billion  
- Market cap: £4.50 × 3.3B = ~£15 billion
```

### Large vs Small Companies

| Category | Market Cap | Examples | Characteristics |
|----------|------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Mega-cap** | >$200B | Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet | Stable, liquid, global |
| **Large-cap** | $10-200B | BP, Shell, Unilever | Blue-chip, dividends |
| **Mid-cap** | $2-10B | Rolls-Royce, Diageo | Growth potential |
| **Small-cap** | <$2B | Emerging companies | High risk/reward |

## 💵 How You Make Money in Stocks

### 1. Capital Gains

Buy low, sell high. Simple in theory, difficult in practice.

```
You buy:    100 shares of BP @ £4.00  = £400
You sell:   100 shares of BP @ £5.00  = £500
Profit:     £100 (25% return)
```

### 2. Dividends

Companies share profits with shareholders:

```
Shell (SHEL) dividend example:
- Annual dividend: ~50p per share
- If you own 1,000 shares: £500/year
- Dividend yield: ~4.5%
```

**Dividend dates:**
- **Ex-dividend** — Buy after this, no dividend
- **Payment date** — Cash hits your account

### 3. Stock Splits

Companies sometimes split shares to improve liquidity:

```
Before split:  1 share @ $1,000
After split:   4 shares @ $250 each
→ Same total value, more affordable to buy
```

## ⚠️ Key Risks

### 1. Price Volatility

Stocks go up and down—sometimes dramatically:

```
UK Market Examples:
• BP crashed 50%+ in 2020 (COVID + oil price war)
• Barclays dropped 40%+ in 2008 (financial crisis)
• Nokia fell 90% from 2007-2013 (smartphone era)
```

### 2. Company Risk

Individual companies can fail entirely:

```
Bankruptcies to remember:
• Wirecard (DE) — €3.2B fraud, collapsed 2020
• Thomas Cook — 178 years, collapsed 2019
• Sears (US) — Once dominant, bankrupt 2018
```

### 3. Market Risk (Systemic)

Entire markets can crash:

```
2008 Financial Crisis:
• S&P 500 fell 57% peak-to-trough
• FTSE 100 fell 49%
• DAX fell 55%
```

### 4. Liquidity Risk

Some stocks are hard to sell:

```
Illiquid stocks:
• Small caps with few traders
• Shares with trading restrictions
→ You might not find a buyer at fair price
```

## 📊 Stock Valuation for Beginners: Understanding the Numbers

You just saw a bunch of numbers on a stock quote. What do they actually mean? Let's break them down like you're learning for the first time.

### What is a "Share" Actually Worth?

A share is just a piece of paper (or digital record) that says you own part of a company. But what's that piece worth?

**The price tag on the stock doesn't tell you if it's cheap or expensive.**

Example:
- Company A: Share price = £10
- Company B: Share price = £100

**Is Company A cheaper?** Not necessarily. You need context.

---

### The Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E) — "How Many Years to Get My Money Back?"

**What it is:** The P/E ratio tells you how many years of current profits it would take to equal the share price.

**The Formula:**
```
P/E Ratio = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)
```

**Real Example: BP vs Tesla**

| Company | Share Price | Earnings Per Share | P/E Ratio | What It Means |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|-----------|---------------|
| **BP** | £4.50 | £0.55 | **8.2** | 8.2 years of profits to equal share price |
| **Tesla** | $250 | $3.50 | **71** | 71 years of profits to equal share price |

**Translation:**
- **BP (P/E 8.2):** Cheap. Investors aren't expecting massive growth. You're buying 8 years of current earnings.
- **Tesla (P/E 71):** Expensive. Investors expect Tesla to grow earnings 10x. You're paying for future growth.

**The Rule of Thumb:**
- **P/E < 10:** Potentially undervalued (but check why — might be a bad company)
- **P/E 15-25:** Reasonable for most companies
- **P/E > 30:** Growth expectations are high (risky if they don't deliver)

**⚠️ Important:** P/E varies by industry. Banks typically trade at P/E 8-12. Tech companies often trade at P/E 25-40+. Compare to similar companies, not across industries.

---

### Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B) — "Am I Paying More Than the Assets Are Worth?"

**What it is:** Compares share price to the company's net assets (what it owns minus what it owes).

**The Formula:**
```
P/B Ratio = Share Price ÷ Book Value Per Share
```

**What is "Book Value"?**
- If BP sold everything it owned (oil rigs, refineries, cash) and paid off all debts, what's left?
- That "net worth" divided by shares = Book Value Per Share

**Real Examples:**

| Company | Share Price | Book Value Per Share | P/B Ratio | Meaning |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Barclays (UK)** | £1.60 | £2.80 | **0.57** | Trading BELOW book value |
| **Apple (US)** | $180 | $12 | **15** | Trading 15x book value |
| **Tesco (UK)** | £2.80 | £2.10 | **1.33** | Trading 33% above book value |

**Translation:**
- **P/B < 1 (like Barclays):** Market thinks assets are worth less than the books say, OR the company is in trouble
- **P/B 1-3:** Normal range
- **P/B > 5:** Market values something beyond physical assets (brand, tech, growth potential)

**⚠️ Warning:** P/B works best for banks and asset-heavy companies. For tech companies with few physical assets, P/B is less meaningful.

---

### Dividend Yield — "How Much Cash Do I Get Back Each Year?"

**What it is:** The annual dividend payment as a percentage of the share price.

**The Formula:**
```
Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend Per Share ÷ Share Price) × 100%
```

**Real Examples:**

| Company | Share Price | Annual Dividend | Dividend Yield | £1,000 Invested = Yearly Cash |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|------------------------------|
| **Shell (SHEL)** | £25 | £1.15 | **4.6%** | £46/year |
| **Vodafone (VOD)** | £0.70 | £0.045 | **6.4%** | £64/year |
| **Meta (META)** | $500 | $2 | **0.4%** | £4/year (low!) |
| **Tesla (TSLA)** | $250 | $0 | **0%** | £0 (no dividend) |

**Translation:**
- **High yield (5%+):** Company pays most profits to shareholders. Good for income, but may mean no growth.
- **Low yield (0-2%):** Company reinvests profits to grow. Good for capital gains, not income.
- **No dividend:** Growth companies or struggling companies.

**⚠️ Trap:** Very high yields (10%+) can signal trouble. If the share price is crashing, the yield looks high but the dividend might get cut.

---

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) — "Is This Company Drowning in Debt?"

**What it is:** Measures how much debt the company has compared to shareholder equity (net worth).

**The Formula:**
```
D/E Ratio = Total Debt ÷ Shareholder Equity
```

**Real Examples:**

| Company | Total Debt | Shareholder Equity | D/E Ratio | Assessment |
|---------|------------|-------------------|-----------|------------|
| **Apple (AAPL)** | $111B | $70B | **1.59** | High, but Apple generates massive cash |
| **BP (BP)** | £55B | £40B | **1.38** | Moderate for oil company |
| **Barclays (BARC)** | £1.2T | £50B | **24** | Very high — banks are different (leverage is normal) |

**The Rule of Thumb:**
- **D/E < 1:** Conservative debt levels
- **D/E 1-2:** Moderate debt (common)
- **D/E > 3:** High debt — higher risk
- **Banks are different:** D/E of 10-20 is normal for banks (they borrow to lend)

**⚠️ Context matters:** Some industries naturally use more debt (utilities, telecoms, real estate).

---

### Return on Equity (ROE) — "How Well Does Management Use My Money?"

**What it is:** Measures how much profit the company generates from shareholder money.

**The Formula:**
```
ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholder Equity × 100%
```

**Translation:** For every £100 of shareholder money, how much profit does the company make?

**Real Examples:**

| Company | ROE | Assessment |
|---------|-----|------------|
| **Apple (AAPL)** | 157% | Exceptional — efficiently uses capital |
| **Microsoft (MSFT)** | 38% | Excellent |
| **BP (BP)** | 18% | Solid for oil company |
| **Barclays (BARC)** | 8% | Mediocre — typical for banks |

**The Rule of Thumb:**
- **ROE > 20%:** Excellent management
- **ROE 10-20%:** Solid performance
- **ROE < 10%:** Weak for most industries
- **Negative ROE:** Losing money

**⚠️ Watch for:** Artificially high ROE from lots of debt (not good operations).

---

### Putting It Together: BP vs Tesla Analysis

| Metric | BP (Oil/Gas) | Tesla (Electric Cars) | What It Tells Us |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|------------------|
| **P/E** | 8.2 | 71 | BP looks cheap; Tesla priced for massive growth |
| **P/B** | 1.4 | 9 | BP near asset value; Tesla valued for future |
| **Dividend Yield** | 4.6% | 0% | BP pays income; Tesla reinvests everything |
| **D/E** | 1.38 | 0.08 | BP uses debt; Tesla is debt-light |
| **ROE** | 18% | 22% | Both are solid, Tesla slightly better |

**Story:**
- **BP** is a mature, income-generating business. Investors don't expect much growth, so it's cheap.
- **Tesla** is a growth story. Investors pay a premium hoping it dominates the future of transport.

Neither is "better" — they serve different investor goals. BP for income, Tesla for growth.

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Equity = ownership** — When you buy stock, you own part of the company

2. **Global markets matter** — FTSE 100, S&P 500, DAX all offer different opportunities

3. **Two ways to make money** — Capital gains (price increase) + Dividends (cash payouts)

4. **Risk is real** — Stocks can crash 50%+; some companies go to zero

5. **Research before buying** — Check P/E, debt, dividends, and company health

6. **Diversification helps** — Don't put all money in one stock or sector
