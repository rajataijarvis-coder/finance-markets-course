## 🤔 The Big Picture

**Commodities** are the raw materials that drive the global economy — oil that powers your car, gold that banks hold, wheat that becomes bread. These physical goods are traded on specialized markets.

Commodities are unique because they're **real assets** — you can touch the gold, use the oil, eat the wheat. This makes them very different from stocks or bonds.

---

## 🌍 What Are Commodities?

### Definition

A commodity is a **fungible good** — one barrel of Brent crude oil is essentially the same as another. This standardization is what makes commodities tradable.

### Categories

```
COMMODITY FAMILIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ENERGY        │ METALS         │ AGRICULTURE    │ LIVESTOCK
──────────────┼────────────────┼────────────────┼──────────────
Oil           │ Gold           │ Wheat          │ Cattle
Natural Gas   │ Silver         │ Corn           │ Hogs
Coal          │ Copper         │ Soybeans       │ 
Electricity   │ Aluminum       │ Coffee         │
              │ Iron ore       │ Cotton         │
              │ Platinum       │ Sugar          │
```

### Hard vs Soft Commodities

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Hard** | Mined or extracted | Oil, gold, copper |
| **Soft** | Grown or raised | Wheat, coffee, cattle |

## 🥇 Precious Metals

### Gold

The most famous commodity:

```
GOLD MARKET FACTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Price (2024): ~$2,350/oz
• Annual production: ~3,500 tonnes
• Largest holders: Central banks, ETFs
```

## 🏛️ How Commodities Trade

### Futures Markets

Most commodities trade on **futures exchanges**:

```
MAJOR COMMODITY EXCHANGES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• ICE (Intercontinental Exchange)
  → Brent oil, gas oil, wheat

• CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange)
  → WTI crude, gold, silver, corn, soybeans

• LME (London Metal Exchange)
  → Copper, aluminum, zinc, nickel

• Euronext Paris
  → Wheat, corn (European focus)
```

### Futures Contracts

```
OIL FUTURES EXAMPLE (BRENT)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Contract: Brent Crude Oil
Exchange: ICE (London)
Size:     1,000 barrels
Months:   Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec (quarterly)
Price:    ~$80/barrel
Value:    $80,000 per contract
```

### Spot vs Forward

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Spot** | Immediate delivery (physical) |
| **Forward** | Future delivery at agreed price |
| **Futures** | Standardized exchange-traded forwards |

## 📈 Investing in Commodities

### Method 1: Direct Ownership

```
PHYSICAL COMMODITIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Buy gold coins/bars from dealers
• Store securely (safe deposit box, vault)
• Pros: Own the real asset
• Cons: Storage costs, illiquid, no income
```

### Method 2: Commodity ETFs

```
COMMODITY ETFs (EXAMPLES)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) — Holds physical gold
• Invesco DB Oil Fund (DBO) — Oil futures
•Aberdeen Standard Physical Gold (SGLA)

How they work:
• ETF buys actual commodity (or futures)
• You get exposure without holding physical
• Price tracks commodity price
```

### Method 3: Mining Stocks

```
COMMODITY STOCKS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Gold miners: Newmont, Barrick Gold, AngloGold
• Oil majors: Shell, BP, TotalEnergies
• Mining companies: BHP, Rio Tinto

Pros: Leverage to commodity prices, dividends
Cons: Operational risks, costs, management
```

### Method 4: Futures Trading

```
DIRECT FUTURES TRADING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Open brokerage account
• Trade futures contracts
• High leverage available
• Requires active management
• Major risk: Contango/roll costs
```

## ⚠️ Key Risks

### 1. Volatility

Commodities are extremely volatile:

```
PRICE EXTREMES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Oil 2020: Went negative (-$37!)
• Coffee 2022: Up 70% in months
• Lithium 2022: Up 300%, then down 70%
• Natural gas 2022: UK up 400%+
```

### 2. Storage Costs

Physical commodities cost money to store:

```
CARRY COSTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Gold storage: ~0.5-1% per year
• Oil storage: $1-3/barrel/month
• Grain storage: Weather dependent

These costs eat into returns
```

### 3. Contango and Backwardation

Futures don't track spot prices perfectly:

```
CONTANGO VS BACKWARDATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Contango (normal):
• Futures price > Spot price
• Roll costs when contract expires

Backwardation (inverted):
• Futures price < Spot price  
• Usually in supply shocks

→ "Roll yield" can be significant
```

### 4. Geopolitical Risk

Commodities are highly exposed:

```
GEOPOLITICAL IMPACT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Ukraine war 2022: Gas +400%, wheat +40%
• Iran sanctions: Oil supply fears
• Chile mining protests: Copper spikes
• Brazil drought: Coffee soaring
```

## 📊 Commodities in a Portfolio

### Role of Commodities

```
DIVERSIFICATION WITH COMMODITIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional: Stocks 60% + Bonds 40%

With commodities:
• Add 5-10% commodities
• Hedge against inflation
• Lower overall portfolio volatility
• Real asset exposure

Historical note:
• 2008: Stocks down 40%, oil up 35%
• 2022: Stocks down, commodities up strongly
```

### Correlation Patterns

```
COMMODITY CORRELATIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Gold ↔ Stocks: Often uncorrelated (diversifier)
• Oil ↔ Stocks: Generally positive correlation
• Dollar ↔ Commodities: Usually inverse
• Ag ↔ Energy: Moderate correlation
```

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Real assets** — Commodities are physical goods you can touch

2. **Four main categories** — Energy, metals, agriculture, livestock

3. **Traded on exchanges** — ICE, CME, LME are major venues

4. **Futures dominate** — Most trading is futures contracts, not physical

5. **Ways to invest** — Physical, ETFs, mining stocks, futures

6. **High volatility** — Prices swing dramatically; leverage amplifies this

7. **Hedge against inflation** — Commodities often rise when currencies fall
