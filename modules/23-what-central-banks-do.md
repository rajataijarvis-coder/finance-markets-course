## 🤔 The Big Question

When you hear "the Fed raised interest rates" or "the Bank of England held rates steady," who's actually making that decision? And why should you care?

The answer: **central banks** — the powerful institutions that control the money supply, set interest rates, and influence virtually every financial decision you make.

---

## 📖 What Is a Central Bank?

A **central bank** is the national institution that:
1. **Controls the money supply** — How much money exists in the economy
2. **Sets interest rates** — The cost of borrowing money
3. **Manages inflation** — Keeps prices stable
4. **Ensures financial stability** — Keeps the banking system working
5. **Issues currency** — Prints money (in modern form, mostly digital)

### Why Central Banks Matter to You

| Your Decision | How Central Banks Affect It |
|---------------|-----------------------------|
| Mortgage rate | Fed/BoE rates directly impact |
| Savings interest | Banks pass central bank rates to savellers |
| Stock prices | Rate changes move markets significantly |
| Job availability | Employment tied to economic health |
| Currency value | Central banks influence exchange rates |

## 🌍 The Major Central Banks

### United States — Federal Reserve (The Fed)

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Founded** | 1913 |
| **Location** | Washington, D.C. |
| **Leader** | Chair (currently Jerome Powell) |
| **Key rates** | Federal Funds Rate |

**Structure:**
- **Board of Governors** — 7 members, appointed by President
- **12 Regional Banks** — New York (most important), Chicago, San Francisco, etc.
- **FOMC** — Federal Open Market Committee (sets monetary policy)

**Dual mandate:** The Fed has two goals:
1. **Maximum employment** — Everyone who wants a job can find one
2. **Price stability** — Keep inflation low and predictable

> 🇺🇸 **The Fed is the most influential central bank** — the US dollar is the world's reserve currency, so Fed decisions affect the entire global economy.

## 🎯 What Central Banks Actually Do

### 1. Set Interest Rates

This is their most visible tool:

```
INTEREST RATE MECHANICS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Central Bank sets:
  → "Base rate" (Fed Funds, Bank Rate, etc.)
  
Banks borrow from central bank at this rate

Banks charge each other (interbank rate)

Banks charge customers (mortgages, loans, savings)

RESULT: Economy slows or speeds up
```

**How rates affect the economy:**

| Higher rates mean | Lower rates mean |
|-------------------|------------------|
| More expensive to borrow | Cheaper to borrow |
| Less spending/spending | More spending/investment |
| Slower inflation | Faster inflation |
| Higher savings rewards | Lower savings rewards |

### 2. Control the Money Supply

Central banks can literally create money:

| Tool | How It Works |
|------|--------------|
| **Open market operations** | Buy/sell government bonds to add/remove money |
| **Quantitative easing (QE)** | Buy bonds on massive scale to add huge money supply |
| **Reserve requirements** | Require banks to hold certain % of deposits (rarely used now) |

### 3. Act as Lender of Last Resort

When banks are in trouble:

- Banks can borrow from central bank in emergencies
- This prevents bank runs and panic
- During 2008 crisis, Fed loaned massively to banks

> 🏦 **Example:** During the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed created the "Term Auction Facility" to lend hundreds of billions to banks that couldn't borrow from each other.

### 4. Ensure Financial Stability

Central banks monitor for risks:

- **Systemic risk** — Risks that could collapse the whole system
- **Bank supervision** — Check banks are sound
- **Macroprudential tools** — Limit excessive lending

### 5. Manage Currency and Exchange Rates

While most central banks let markets set exchange rates, they can intervene:

- **Buy/sell their own currency** to influence rates
- **Raise/lower rates** to attract foreign investment
- **Currency reserves** — Hold foreign currency to intervene

## ⚖️ Independence and Accountability

### Why Independence Matters

Central banks should be independent from political pressure:

- **Politicians** might want low rates before elections
- **Independent banks** can make unpopular decisions
- **Independence** builds credibility

> 📜 **In practice:** Central banks are independent but accountable. Fed Chair testifies to Congress. BoE Governor faces Parliament. ECB President answers to EU Parliament.

### The Trade-off

| Political control | Independent central bank |
|-------------------|-------------------------|
| Short-term fixes | Long-term stability |
| Inflation temptation | Credibility |
| Populist policies | Unpopular decisions |

**Most countries** now favor independent central banks with clear mandates.

## 🔄 Recent Evolution

### The 2008 Crisis

- Central banks cut rates to zero
- Started QE (buying bonds)
- Became " lenders of last resort"

### The 2020 Crisis (COVID)

- Rates cut to zero again
- QE expanded massively
- New programs: Fed buys corporate bonds, ETFs

### The 2022-2024 Inflation Surge

- Inflation spiked globally
- Central banks raised rates aggressively
- Fastest rate hikes in decades (Fed, BoE, ECB)
- Started "quantitative tightening" (QT)

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Central banks control the money supply** — and set interest rates that affect everything from mortgages to stocks

2. **Major central banks:** Fed (US), BoE (UK), ECB (Eurozone), BoJ (Japan)

3. **Fed's dual mandate:** Maximum employment and price stability

4. **Tools:** Interest rates, open market operations, QE, lender of last resort

5. **Independence** — Central banks should be free from political pressure to maintain credibility

6. **Global impact** — The Fed's decisions ripple through the entire world economy

7. **Recent history:** 2008 crisis → QE; 2020 crisis → massive QE; 2022-24 → rate hikes to fight inflation
