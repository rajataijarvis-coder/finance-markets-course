## 🤔 The Big Question

You've probably heard "the central bank's inflation target is 2%." But what does that actually mean? Why 2%? And what happens when inflation spikes to 10% or falls to 0%?

The answer: **inflation targeting** — the framework central banks use to guide the economy toward stable prices.

---

## 📖 What Is Inflation?

**Inflation** is when prices rise over time, so each unit of currency buys less:

```
YOUR MONEY OVER TIME
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

2020: £10 buys you a full lunch
2024: £10 buys you a smaller lunch

Why? Inflation — prices went up, your money bought less
```

### How Inflation Is Measured

| Metric | What It Measures |
|--------|-------------------|
| **CPI (Consumer Price Index)** | Cost of a basket of goods (food, rent, transport) |
| **RPI (Retail Price Index)** | Broader measure including housing costs (UK) |
| **PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)** | Fed's preferred measure (US) |
| **Core inflation** | Inflation excluding food and energy (less volatile) |

### Inflation Examples (2024)

| Country | Inflation Rate |
|---------|----------------|
| **UK** | ~2-3% |
| **US** | ~2.5-3% |
| **Eurozone** | ~2% |
| **Japan** | ~2% |
| **Emerging markets** | Varies widely (e.g., Brazil 4%, Turkey 50%+) |

---

## 📊 What Is Inflation Targeting?

**Inflation targeting** is when a central bank explicitly aims for a specific inflation rate:

| Central Bank | Target | Current Status |
|--------------|--------|----------------|
| **Fed (US)** | 2% | Now explicitly targets 2% |
| **BoE (UK)** | 2% | 2% target (flexible) |
| **ECB (Eurozone)** | 2% | Symmetric target |
| **BoJ (Japan)** | 2% | Price stability goal |
| **RBA (Australia)** | 2-3% | Target range |

### The Framework

```
INFLATION TARGETING FRAMEWORK
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. SET TARGET
   Central bank announces: "2% inflation"

2. FORECAST
   Predict inflation based on economy

3. ADJUST POLICY
   If inflation too high → raise rates
   If inflation too low → cut rates

4. COMMUNICATE
   Explain decisions to markets/public

5. REPORT
   Publish inflation reports, explain deviations
```

### Why 2%?

| Target | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| **Not 0%** | Small positive inflation encourages spending/investment |
| **Not high** | High inflation erodes savings, hurts fixed-income |
| **2% is standard** | Balance — low enough for stability, high enough for flexibility |

> 🏹 **Flexible targeting:** Modern central banks don't hit 2% exactly. They aim for it "symmetrically" — overshooting as bad as undershooting.

## 🔄 The Inflation Targeting Framework

### Step 1: Forecasting

Central banks build inflation forecasts:

```
INFLATION FORECAST (simplified)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Factors considered:
• Current inflation
• Energy prices
• Food prices  
• Wages
• Exchange rates
• Global trade

Output:
• "We forecast inflation at 3.5% in 12 months"
• "We forecast inflation at 2.2% in 24 months"
```

### Step 2: Policy Response

| Inflation Scenario | Central Bank Response |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Above target** | Raise rates, tighten policy |
| **At target** | Maintain current policy |
| **Below target** | Cut rates, ease policy |
| **Way below target** | Aggressive cuts, QE |

### Step 3: Communication

Central banks publish:

- **Inflation reports** (e.g., UK's "Inflation Report")
- **Meeting minutes** (Fed minutes, BoE minutes)
- **Press conferences** (Fed Chair press conference)
- **Projections** (economic projections including inflation)

## 📈 What Happens When Inflation Is Too High?

### The Problem

High inflation:

- Eats away savings
- Creates uncertainty
- Hurts fixed-income retirees
- Can spiral into hyperinflation
- Forces central bank to act aggressively

### Historical Examples

| Period | Inflation | Central Bank Response |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|
| **UK 1970s** | 25%+ | Huge rate hikes, controls |
| **US 1970s** | 15%+ | Volcker Fed raised rates to 20% |
| **2021-2023** | 10%+ (UK/US/EU) | Fastest rate hikes in decades |

### How Central Banks Fight High Inflation

```
FIGHTING INFLATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. RAISE INTEREST RATES
   Higher rates → less borrowing → less spending

2. TIGHTEN POLICY
   Reduce money supply, raise reserve requirements

3. FORWARD GUIDANCE
   "Higher for longer" to anchor expectations

4. QT (Quantitative Tightening)
   Stop buying bonds, let balance sheet shrink

RESULT: Slower economy, lower inflation
```

> 📉 **The cost:** Fighting inflation usually causes a recession. The 1980s recessions were the price of beating 1970s inflation.

---

## 📉 What Happens When Inflation Is Too Low (or Negative)?

### The Problem

Low or negative inflation (deflation):

- People delay purchases (waiting for更低 prices)
- Debt becomes more expensive in real terms
- Companies cut prices, then wages
- Can spiral into deflationary spiral
- Harder to escape than inflation

### Historical Examples

| Period | Country/Region | Deflation |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| **1930s** | Global | Great Depression |
| **1990s** | Japan | Lost decade |
| **2015-2020** | Eurozone | Near-zero inflation |
| **2020** | Global | COVID deflation (temporary) |

### How Central Banks Fight Low Inflation

```
FIGHTING DEFLATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. CUT RATES TO ZERO
   Make borrowing cheap

2. QE (Quantitative Easing)
   Buy bonds, add money to economy

3. FORWARD GUIDANCE
   "Rates will stay low for a long time"

4. NEGATIVE RATES (unconventional)
   Charge banks to hold reserves

5. DIRECT INTERVENTION
   Buy stocks, corporate bonds (extreme)
```

## 🌍 Inflation Targeting Around the World

### The Pioneers: New Zealand (1990)

New Zealand was the first to adopt explicit inflation targeting:

- Target: 0-2% initially
- Gave central bank independence
- Successfully controlled inflation

### The UK Experience

| Period | Inflation Target | Outcome |
|--------|------------------|---------|
| **1992-2003** | 2.5% | Relatively successful |
| **2003-2021** | 2% | Good control |
| **2021-2023** | 2% | Missed badly (10%+) |
| **2023-present** | 2% | Returning toward target |

### The US Approach

- Fed didn't formally adopt 2% target until 2012
- Previously had "dual mandate" but no explicit target
- Now: "2% inflation target, symmetric"
- More flexible than other central banks

### The Eurozone

- ECB adopted 2% target in 2021
- Previously had "below but close to 2%"
- "Symmetric" means 2% is midpoint, not ceiling

### Japan — The Exception

- BoJ struggled for decades with low inflation
- Tried massive QE, negative rates
- Inflation finally rising in 2023-2024
- Target: "2%, stably"

## 📊 The Inflation of 2021-2024

### What Happened

```
INFLATION SURGE (2021-2022)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

• COVID pandemic ends
• Supply chains disrupted
• Energy prices spike (Russia-Ukraine)
• Governments stimulate economies
• Demand surges faster than supply
• Inflation goes from 2% to 10%+

Central bank response:
• Fastest rate hikes in decades
• Aggressive tightening
• 2023-2024: Inflation falling
```

### Key Learnings

| Lesson | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Globalization can break** | Supply chains disrupted |
| **Energy matters** | Oil/gas prices drive inflation |
| **Central banks can act** | Hiked aggressively despite political pressure |
| **Inflation returns** | Can't assume it stays low forever |
| **2% is hard** | Even with good policy, hitting 2% is difficult |

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Inflation targeting** is when central banks explicitly aim for a specific inflation rate (usually 2%)

2. **Why 2%?** — Low enough for stability, high enough for flexibility; not zero because some inflation encourages spending

3. **Above target** → central banks raise rates; **below target** → they cut rates

4. **The 2021-2024 inflation surge** was global, caused by supply chain issues + energy prices + stimulus

5. **Fighting high inflation has costs** — higher rates can cause recessions

6. **Low inflation is also problematic** — can lead to deflationary spirals

7. **Communication is key** — forward guidance shapes expectations
