## 🤔 The Big Question

When you hear "the pound fell against the dollar" or "the euro is weakening," what does that actually mean? And more importantly — who's responsible for the value of a country's currency?

The answer: **currency policy** — the tools and decisions that determine exchange rates, and why they matter for your investments.

---

## 📖 What Is a Currency?

A **currency** is a country's official money:

| Currency | Code | Countries |
|----------|------|-----------|
| **US Dollar** | USD | United States |
| **British Pound** | GBP | United Kingdom |
| **Euro** | EUR | Eurozone (20 countries) |
| **Japanese Yen** | JPY | Japan |
| **Swiss Franc** | CHF | Switzerland |

### Exchange Rates

An **exchange rate** is simply the price of one currency in terms of another:

| Pair | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **GBP/USD** | How many dollars for £1 | £1 = $1.27 |
| **EUR/USD** | How many dollars for €1 | €1 = $1.08 |
| **GBP/EUR** | How many euros for £1 | £1 = €1.18 |

> 📊 **Convention:** In forex, the first currency is the "base," second is the "quote." GBP/USD means GBP is base, USD is quote — £1 = $1.27.

---

## 🌍 Exchange Rate Systems

### 1. Floating Exchange Rates

**How it works:** The market determines the exchange rate based on supply and demand.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Automatic adjustment | Volatile |
| No need for central bank intervention | Uncertainty for businesses |
| Markets reflect fundamentals | Can overshoot |

**Countries with floating:** US, UK, Japan, Canada, Australia

### 2. Fixed Exchange Rates

**How it works:** The central bank commits to maintaining a specific exchange rate.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Stability | Requires large reserves |
| Predictability | Hard to maintain |
| Reduces speculation | May conflict with domestic policy |

**Countries with fixed/pegged:** Saudi Arabia (riyal pegged to USD), Hong Kong (dollar pegged to USD)

### 3. Managed Floating

**How it works:** Mostly float, but central bank intervenes occasionally.

Most major economies use this hybrid approach.

## 🎯 How Central Banks Influence Currency

### 1. Interest Rates

Higher rates → stronger currency (attracts foreign investment):

```
RATE HIKE → CURRENCY STRENGTHENS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Fed raises rates
  ↓
US bonds offer higher returns
  ↓
Foreign investors buy USD to invest
  ↓
Demand for USD increases
  ↓
USD strengthens vs. other currencies
```

### 2. Direct Intervention

Central banks can buy or sell their own currency:

| Action | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Buy own currency** | Currency strengthens |
| **Sell own currency** | Currency weakens |

**Example:** If the pound is too weak, BoE can buy GBP:

```
BOE INTERVENTION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

BoE sells foreign currency (euros, dollars)
  ↓
Receives pounds in exchange
  ↓
Reduces pounds in circulation
  ↓
Pound strengthens
```

### 3. Capital Controls

Governments can restrict money flowing in/out:

| Control | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **Tax on foreign purchases** | Discourages currency outflow |
| **Limits on overseas investing** | Keeps money domestic |
| **Require repatriation** | Bring foreign earnings home |

**Examples:** China has capital controls; during crises, countries sometimes reimpose controls.

### 4. Currency Reserves

Central banks hold reserves of foreign currencies:

- **US dollar reserves** — to intervene if needed
- **Euro, yen, pound reserves** — diversified
- **Gold reserves** — traditional safe haven

**Why it matters:** Large reserves = ability to intervene in currency markets.

## 📈 Why Currency Matters

### For Investors

| Currency Movement | Impact on Your Portfolio |
|------------------|---------------------------|
| GBP strengthens | Foreign investments worth less in GBP |
| GBP weakens | Foreign investments worth more in GBP |
| USD strengthens | US assets perform better in GBP terms |

**Example:**

```
YOUR US STOCKS IN GBP TERMS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

January: Apple at $150 = £120
          (GBP/USD = 1.25)

June: Apple at $150 = £118
      (GBP/USD = 1.27 → GBP stronger)

Same Apple price, but less GBP!
```

### For Businesses

- Import costs change with exchange rates
- Export competitiveness changes
- Profit margins affected by currency movements
- Hedging becomes necessary

### For Economies

| Currency | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Too strong** | Exports expensive, imports cheap |
| **Too weak** | Imports expensive, inflation risk |
| **Volatile** | Hard for businesses to plan |

## 🌍 Major Currencies in the World

### The US Dollar — Global Reserve Currency

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Reserve currency status** | Most countries hold USD as primary reserve |
| **Trade invoicing** | Most global trade in USD |
| **Commodities** | Oil, gold priced in USD |
| **Impact** | Fed policy affects whole world |

**Why it matters:** When the Fed raises rates, it strengthens the dollar — which can hurt emerging markets with USD debt.

### The Euro — Second Global Currency

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Second most traded** | Major global currency |
| **20 countries** | One currency for Eurozone |
| **Reserve holdings** | Growing but far behind USD |

### The British Pound

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Third most traded** | Major global currency |
| **Historical significance** | Former empire, strong financial sector |
| **Volatility** | Can move significantly on news |

### The Japanese Yen

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Low-yield currency** | Often used for "carry trades" |
| **Safe haven** | Strengthens in crises |
| **Big forex reserves** | Japan holds massive USD reserves |

### The Chinese Yuan (Renminbi)

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Growing importance** | Internationalizing slowly |
| **Controlled** | Not fully market-determined |
| **Reserve currency** | Added to IMF reserves (2016) |

## 🏦 Currency Policy in Practice

### The UK Approach

- **Floating exchange rate** — market determines GBP value
- **No formal target** — no explicit GBP target
- **Occasional intervention** — BoE can intervene but rarely does
- **Inflation targeting** — policy focuses on inflation, not currency

### The US Approach

- **Strong dollar policy** — US prefers strong dollar (theoretically)
- **Market-determined** — rarely intervenes
- **Treasury policy** — Treasury, not Fed, officially sets currency policy

### The Eurozone Approach

- **Single currency** — 20 countries share the euro
- **ECB sets policy** — for entire Eurozone
- **No separate currency policy** — can't devalue to help exports

### China — The Exception

- **Managed float** — tightly controlled exchange rate
- **Capital controls** — restrictions on money flows
- **Trade surplus** — accumulates USD reserves
- **Gradual internationalization** — yuan added to IMF reserves

## 📊 Recent Currency Events

### The 2022-2023 Dollar Strength

```
DOLLAR STRENGTH (2022)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Fed raised rates aggressively
  ↓
USD strengthened significantly
  
GBP/USD fell from 1.38 to 1.05
EUR/USD fell from 1.15 to 0.98
USD/JPY went from 115 to 160

Impact:
• Imported inflation (weaker currency)
• Emerging markets struggled (USD debt)
• US assets performed well for UK investors
```

### Brexit and the Pound

```
POUND FALLS AFTER BREXIT (2016)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

GBP/USD: 1.50 → 1.20 (20% fall)

Causes:
• Uncertainty
• Trade concerns
• Scotland independence fears

Long-term: Pound recovered somewhat but remains 
below pre-Brexit levels
```

### Yen Weakness and Intervention

```
JAPAN'S YEN (2022-2024)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

USD/JPY: 115 → 160 (Yen weakness)

BoJ maintained low rates while others raised
  ↓
Yen weakened significantly
  
Japan intervened (sold USD, bought yen) in 2022
  ↓
Limited success
```

## 💡 Key Takeaways

1. **Exchange rates** are the price of one currency in terms of another

2. **Floating rates** are determined by markets; **fixed rates** are maintained by central banks

3. **Interest rates** are the primary tool — higher rates strengthen currency

4. **Direct intervention** — central banks can buy/sell currency but rarely do

5. **The US dollar is the global reserve currency** — Fed decisions affect currencies worldwide

6. **Currency matters for investors** — exchange rates affect returns on foreign investments

7. **GBP is a major floating currency** — third most traded globally

8. **China is an exception** — tightly manages the yuan, uses capital controls
