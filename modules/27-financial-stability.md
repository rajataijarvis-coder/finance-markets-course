## 🤔 Why This Matters

Remember the 2008 financial crisis? Or the 2022 UK pension crisis? Or the 2023 banking turmoil in the US and Europe?

These weren't just "bad days" for markets. They were **systemic risks** — threats that could collapse the entire financial system.

In this module, we'll explore:
- What makes a financial system stable (or unstable)
- The tools central banks and regulators use to prevent crises
- How to think about systemic risk as an investor

---

## 🏗️ What is Financial Stability?

Financial stability means the financial system can:
- **Absorb shocks** (like a sudden interest rate hike or a bank failure)
- **Allocate capital efficiently** (money flows to productive uses)
- **Maintain confidence** (people trust their money is safe)

Think of it like a city's power grid. A stable grid doesn't fail when everyone turns on their heaters at once. A stable financial system doesn't collapse when markets drop 20%.

### The Opposite: Financial Instability

When stability breaks down:
- Banks stop lending → businesses can't get financing
- Markets freeze → no one can buy or sell
- Confidence evaporates → bank runs
- Wealth destroys → pensions and savings evaporate

**Real examples:**
- **2008 Global Crisis:** Lehman Brothers collapsed, triggering a worldwide credit freeze
- **2016 UK pension crisis:** Liability-driven investment (LDI) strategies collapsed when gilt yields spiked
- **2023 US regional banks:** Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) failed after rapid rate hikes
- **2023 Credit Suisse:** Confidence crisis led to emergency takeover by UBS

## 🔴 The Three Pillars of Instability

### Pillar 1: Excessive Leverage

Leverage is borrowing money to amplify returns. It works great... until it doesn't.

**How it creates instability:**
- Small losses → magnified through leverage → big losses
- When everyone deleverages at once → markets crash
- Banks with high leverage can fail even from small losses

**Real example: Lehman Brothers (2008)**
Lehman had **30:1 leverage** — for every $1 of capital, they had $30 in assets. When mortgage bonds lost just 3% of their value, Lehman's capital was wiped out.

**Real example: SVB (2023)**
SVB had huge unrealized losses on long-dated government bonds. When interest rates rose rapidly, those bonds lost value. SVB's equity buffer was too thin to absorb the losses.

### Pillar 2: Maturity Transformation

This is a fancy term for: **borrowing short, lending long**.

Banks take deposits (payable on demand) and lend out money (fixed for 20-30 years). This works fine... until everyone wants their money back at once.

**The classic bank run:**
```
Day 1: Rumors spread that Bank XYZ is in trouble
Day 2: Depositors withdraw billions in a panic
Day 3: Bank runs out of cash → forced to sell assets at fire-sale prices
Day 4: Bank fails → contagion spreads to other banks
```

**Real example: Northern Rock (2007, UK)**
The first bank run in the UK in 150 years. Queues formed outside branches as people withdrew savings. The UK government had to step in and guarantee all deposits.

### Pillar 3: Interconnectedness

Financial institutions are linked like a web. When one fails, the shock travels to others.

```
        Bank A
       /     \
    Hedge    Insurance
    Fund      Company
       \     /
        Bank B
```

**How contagion works:**
- Bank A fails → can't pay what it owes to Hedge Fund
- Hedge Fund loses money → can't meet obligations to Insurance Company
- Insurance Company → can't pay claims to Bank B
- Bank B fails → domino effect continues

**Real example: AIG (2008)**
AIG sold insurance on mortgage bonds ("credit default swaps"). When those bonds lost value, AIG owed billions. AIG's failure would have destroyed dozens of major banks that were its counterparties. The US government bailed AIG out to prevent contagion.

## 🛡️ Tools for Maintaining Stability

### 1. Capital Requirements

Banks must hold enough capital to absorb losses. This is like a shock absorber.

**The Basel Accords (global standards):**
- **Basel III (post-2008):** Banks must hold more capital
- **Minimum CET1 ratio:** 4.5% of risk-weighted assets
- **Capital Conservation Buffer:** Additional 2.5% (total minimum ~7%)
- **Liquidity Coverage Ratio:** Enough high-quality liquid assets to survive 30 days of stress

**UK example:** The Bank of England's Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) enforces Basel III in the UK.

**US example:** The Federal Reserve and OCC supervise large banks under Basel III.

**Europe example:** The European Central Bank (ECB) directly supervises major eurozone banks.

### 2. Stress Testing

Banks must simulate "worst-case scenarios" to prove they can survive crises.

**What happens in a stress test:**
1. Regulators design a severe recession scenario
2. Banks calculate losses under that scenario
3. Banks prove they have enough capital to survive
4. If they fail → must raise capital or cut dividends

**Real examples:**
- **UK:** The Bank of England's Annual Cyclical Scenario (ACS)
- **US:** The Federal Reserve's Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review (CCAR)
- **Europe:** ECB's Stress Test (covers ~70% of eurozone banking assets)

### 3. Resolution Plans ("Living Wills")

Large banks must have a plan for how they could be wound down without causing chaos.

**Why this matters:**
- Too big to fail = too big to exist
- If a bank has a clear resolution plan, markets trust it can fail safely
- Forces banks to simplify their structures

### 4. Macroprudential Tools

These are tools that target the entire system, not just individual banks:

| Tool | What it does | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| Counter-cyclical capital buffer | Require banks to hold more capital in good times | UK BoE raised CCyB to 2% in 2023 |
| Loan-to-value limits | Cap how much you can borrow to buy a house | UK: 95% LTV limits for first-time buyers |
| Loan-to-income limits | Cap debt payments as % of income | UK: 4.5x income limit on mortgages |
| Leverage ratios | Simple limit on total borrowing | UK: 3% minimum leverage ratio |

### 5. Central Bank Liquidity Facilities

When markets freeze, central banks step in as the "lender of last resort."

**How it works:**
- Banks with good collateral can borrow from the central bank
- This prevents fire sales of assets
- Maintains confidence that cash is available

**Real examples:**
- **Bank of England:** Extended Term Funding Scheme (TFSME) during COVID
- **Federal Reserve:** Discount Window + emergency facilities (PMCCF, SMCCF in 2020)
- **ECB:** Targeted Longer-Term Refinancing Operations (TLTROs)

## 📊 The Financial Stability Cycle

Financial stability isn't a fixed state — it's a continuous cycle:

```
      Expansion
     /         \
    ↓           ↑  (Boom)
  Low           High
  Risk          Risk
    ↑           ↓  (Bust)
  Crisis        Recovery
     \         /
      Contraction
```

### The Boom Phase
- Asset prices rise
- Everyone feels wealthy
- Risk appetite increases
- Leverage builds quietly
- Warning signs appear...

### The Bust Phase
- Asset prices fall
- Leverage amplifies losses
- Interconnectedness spreads the pain
- Confidence collapses
- Credit freezes

### The Crisis Phase
- Central banks intervene
- Some institutions fail
- Contagion is contained (hopefully)
- New regulations emerge

### The Recovery Phase
- Confidence returns
- Credit starts flowing again
- Regulations are implemented
- The cycle begins again...

> 💡 **Key insight:** The crisis is often the catalyst for reform. Basel III came from 2008. Post-2023 reforms are still being implemented.

## 🎯 What This Means for You

As an investor, understanding financial stability helps you:

### 1. Spot Risk Early
When leverage is high and regulation is light — be cautious
- Watch for signs of credit expansion
- Monitor central bank rhetoric on "financial stability"

### 2. Understand Black Swan Events
Some crises are predictable in hindsight:
- 2008: Subprime mortgages + high leverage
- 2022: LDI strategies ignored interest rate risk
- 2023: Regional banks didn't hedge rate risk

### 3. Protect Your Portfolio
- Don't over-leverage yourself
- Diversify across institutions
- Hold some cash/liquidity
- Understand your exposures

### 4. Time the Cycle
Financial conditions tighten before recessions:
- Central banks raise rates → credit slows → asset prices fall
- Monitor leading indicators (credit growth, yield curve, volatility)

## 🌎 Global Examples: Financial Stability in Action

### United Kingdom
- **BoE Financial Policy Committee (FPC):** Monitors systemic risk
- **Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA):** Supervises banks and insurers
- **2022 LDI Crisis:** UK pension funds used leverage to hedge long-term liabilities. When gilt yields spiked, margin calls forced forced selling. The BoE had to intervene and buy gilts to stabilize the market.

### United States
- **Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC):** Designates "systemically important" institutions
- **Federal Reserve:** Conducts stress tests (CCAR)
- **FDIC:** Insures deposits up to $250,000
- **2023 Regional Bank Crisis:** SVB, Signature Bank, and First Republic failed within weeks. Fed created emergency lending facilities to prevent broader panic.

### Europe
- **European Central Bank (ECB):** Directly supervises ~100 major banks
- **European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB):** Monitors macroprudential risk
- **2023 Credit Suisse:** After losses and confidence issues, UBS acquired Credit Suisse in an emergency deal backed by Swiss authorities and the ECB.

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Financial stability** = the system can absorb shocks and continue functioning

2. **Three pillars of instability:**
   - Excessive leverage
   - Maturity transformation (short-term borrowing, long-term lending)
   - Interconnectedness (contagion between institutions)

3. **Stability tools:**
   - Capital requirements (Basel III)
   - Stress tests
   - Resolution plans
   - Macroprudential tools (LTV limits, counter-cyclical buffers)
   - Central bank liquidity facilities

4. **The stability cycle:** Expansion → Bust → Crisis → Recovery → Expansion

5. **For investors:** Understand systemic risk, don't over-leverage, monitor financial conditions
