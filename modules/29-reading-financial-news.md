## 🤔 The Big Question

Financial news is everywhere — Bloomberg, FT, WSJ, CNBC, BBC, Google, Twitter, Reddit. But how do you read it effectively?

Most people read financial news wrong. They:
- Focus on the wrong details
- Miss the actual story
- React to noise instead of signals
- Get confused by jargon

This module will teach you to read financial news like a pro — quickly extracting what's important and ignoring the rest.

---

## 📖 The Three Layers of Financial News

Every financial news story has three layers:

### Layer 1: The Facts
What happened? (This is what most people focus on)

### Layer 2: The Context
Why did it happen? What does it mean?

### Layer 3: The Implication
What should I do about it?

> 💡 **Key insight:** Most people stop at Layer 1. To profit from financial news, you need to reach Layer 3.

---

## 🔍 How to Read Different Types of Stories

### Type 1: Market Moving News

**What it is:** News that directly impacts asset prices

**Examples:**
- Central bank interest rate decisions
- Earnings reports from major companies
- Geopolitical events (wars, elections, trade deals)
- Economic data releases (GDP, jobs, inflation)

**How to read it:**

```
1. WHAT happened?
   - Rate cut: 25bps to 4.5%
   - Apple beat earnings by 12%

2. WHY did it happen?
   - Inflation falling faster than expected
   - iPhone sales strong in China

3. MARKET REACTION:
   - Did markets already price this in?
   - Is the move bigger/smaller than expected?

4. WHAT'S NEXT?
   - What does this mean for future decisions?
   - What are the second-order effects?
```

**Real example:**

```
HEADLINE: "Fed Raises Rates by 25 Basis Points"

Layer 1 (Facts): Fed raised rates from 5.25% to 5.50%. This is the 11th rate hike since 2022.

Layer 2 (Context): The Fed is continuing to fight inflation. But the statement dropped language about "ongoing increases."

Layer 3 (Implication): This may be the last rate hike. Bond markets will rally. Growth stocks will benefit.
```

### Type 2: Company News

**What it is:** News specific to a company or sector

**Examples:**
- Earnings reports
- Product launches
- Executive changes
- Regulatory issues
- M&A announcements

**How to read it:**

```
1. FINANCIAL IMPACT:
   - How does this affect revenue, profits, or cash flow?
   - Is the impact one-time or ongoing?

2. COMPETITIVE POSITION:
   - Does this make the company stronger or weaker vs competitors?
   - Any moat changes (patents, market share, brand)?

3. VALUATION IMPACT:
   - Does this change the long-term value of the company?
   - How much is this worth per share?
```

**Real example:**

```
HEADLINE: "Shell Reports Record Q4 Profits, Announces $5 Billion Buyback"

Layer 1: Shell earned $8.2 billion in Q4 2023, up 45% year-over-year.
         Announced $5 billion share buyback program.

Layer 2: Oil prices were elevated due to geopolitical disruptions.
         Strong free cash flow allows capital returns to shareholders.

Layer 3: Good for Shell shareholders. Signals confidence in oil prices.
         But climate concerns may pressure long-term demand.
```

### Type 3: Economic Data

**What it is:** Macroeconomic indicators

**Examples:**
- GDP growth
- Employment reports
- Inflation (CPI, PPI)
- Retail sales
- Consumer confidence

**How to read it:**

```
1. THE NUMBER:
   - What was the actual result?
   - What was expected?

2. THE TREND:
   - Is this getting better or worse?
   - How does it compare to recent history?

3. POLICY IMPLICATIONS:
   - What will the Fed/BoE/ECB do next?
   - How will this affect rates, QE, fiscal policy?

4. MARKET REACTION:
   - Did markets already expect this?
   - Is this a surprise?
```

**Real example:**

```
HEADLINE: "US Jobs Report: 250,000 New Jobs, Unemployment at 3.4%"

Layer 1: February 2024 jobs data exceeded expectations (200k forecast).
         Unemployment at 50-year low.

Layer 2: Labor market remains exceptionally strong.
         Wage growth moderated (good for inflation).

Layer 3: Fed has room to keep rates higher for longer.
         Bad news for bonds, mixed for stocks.
```

### Type 4: Geopolitical / Structural News

**What it is:** News about politics, policy, or global events

**Examples:**
- Elections
- Trade wars
- Sanctions
- Climate policy
- Regulatory changes

**How to read it:**

```
1. DIRECT IMPACT:
   - Which sectors/companies are affected immediately?

2. SECOND-ORDER EFFECTS:
   - What are the downstream consequences?
   - Supply chain effects? Cost effects?

3. UNCERTAINTY:
   - How long will this last?
   - How unpredictable is this?

4. HISTORICAL PARALLEL:
   - Has anything like this happened before?
   - How did markets react?
```

## 🎯 How to Process News Efficiently

### Step 1: Scan First
- Read the headline
- Check if it's market-moving
- Skip if not relevant to your interests

### Step 2: Read the Lede
- First paragraph contains the story
- If the lede doesn't interest you, skip the rest

### Step 3: Check the Details
- Only read further if it's relevant
- Look for numbers, dates, specific impacts

### Step 4: Form an Opinion
- What do you think happened?
- What's the market not seeing?

### Step 5: Take Action (or Don't)
- Do nothing if there's no actionable insight
- Adjust positions if there is
- Set alerts if you're waiting on something

## 🔑 Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Reacting to Headlines
**Wrong:** "Fed raised rates! Sell everything!"
**Right:** Check if this was expected. Look at the statement. Assess what'sPriced in.

### Mistake 2: Confirmation Bias
**Wrong:** Only reading sources that agree with your position
**Right:** Seek out contrary views. Challenge your assumptions.

### Mistake 3: Noise vs. Signal
**Wrong:** Reacting to every tweet and rumor
**Right:** Focus on primary sources and verified information.

### Mistake 4: Chasing the Story
**Wrong:** "Everyone's talking about AI stocks, I should buy some"
**Right:** Have your own thesis. Don't follow the crowd.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring the Disclaimer
**Wrong:** Taking analyst price targets as gospel
**Right:** Remember analysts often have conflicts of interest.

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Three layers to every story:** Facts → Context → Implication

2. **Different story types require different analysis:**
   - Market moving: What's priced in? What's next?
   - Company: Financial impact + competitive position
   - Economic data: Policy implications + trends
   - Geopolitical: Direct + second-order effects

3. **Quality over quantity:**
   - Read fewer sources, read them deeply
   - Primary sources > social media

4. **Don't react — process:**
   - Scan → Read lede → Check details → Form opinion → Act (or don't)

5. **Common mistakes:**
   - Reacting to headlines
   - Confirmation bias
   - Confusing noise and signal
   - Chasing the story
