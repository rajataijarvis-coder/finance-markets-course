## 🤔 Why Economic Indicators Matter

When the US jobs report comes out, markets move. When UK inflation data is released, the Bank of England pays attention. But why?

Economic indicators are the **heartbeat monitors** of the economy. They tell us:
- Is the economy growing or shrinking?
- Is inflation rising or falling?
- Should central banks raise or lower interest rates?

In this module, we'll break down the most important economic indicators, where to find them, and how to interpret them.

---

## 📊 The Indicator Hierarchy

Economic indicators can be grouped by how they fit into the economic cycle:

```
                Expansion
               /          \
             ↓              ↓
      Leading Indicators  Coincident Indicators
             ↓              ↓
             ↓              ↓
         Recession       Lagging Indicators
               \          /
                Contraction
```

### Leading Indicators
**What they are:** Predict the future direction of the economy  
**Examples:** Stock markets, building permits, consumer confidence  
**Use:** Forecast turning points

### Coincident Indicators
**What they are:** Tell us what's happening right now  
**Examples:** GDP, employment, retail sales  
**Use:** Confirm the current state

### Lagging Indicators
**What they are:** Confirm what happened in the past  
**Examples:** Unemployment rate, corporate profits, interest rates  
**Use:** Verify trends (after the fact)

> 💡 **Key insight:** Leading indicators are most valuable for investors because they predict what's coming.

## 🌍 The Big Five Indicators

### 1. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

**What it is:** Total value of goods and services produced in a country

**How it's measured:**
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
Where:
C = Consumer spending (70% of economy!)
I = Investment
G = Government spending
X = Exports
M = Imports
```

**Why it matters:**
- Growth = good (usually)
- Negative growth = recession
- Per capita GDP = standard of living

**The numbers:**

| Country | GDP (2023) | Growth Rate |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| United States | $26.9 trillion | ~2.5% |
| China | $17.7 trillion | ~5.2% |
| Japan | $4.2 trillion | ~1.9% |
| Germany | $4.4 trillion | ~-0.1% |
| United Kingdom | $3.3 trillion | ~0.1% |

**When released:**
- US: First estimate ~4 weeks after quarter end
- UK: First estimate ~6 weeks after quarter end
- Final: Revised twice over 3 months

### 2. Employment / Jobs Report

**What it is:** Monthly snapshot of the labor market

**Key numbers:**
- **Nonfarm Payrolls (NFP):** Jobs added/lost (US)
- **Unemployment Rate:** % of workforce without jobs
- **Average Hourly Earnings:** Wage growth

**Why it matters:**
- Employment = consumer spending = economy
- Wage growth drives inflation
- Central banks obsess over this data

**The US Jobs Report (first Friday of each month):**
```
What to watch:
- NFP: +250k (strong) vs -100k (weak)
- Unemployment: 3.4% (tight) vs 6.0% (weak)
- Wage growth: 4.0% YoY (hot) vs 2.0% YoY (cold)
```

**UK equivalent:** ONS Labour Force Survey (monthly)

**European equivalent:** Eurostat Labour Force Survey

### 3. Inflation (CPI / PPI)

**What it is:** The rate at which prices rise

**Key measures:**

| Indicator | What it measures | Example |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| CPI | Consumer prices (what you pay) | Milk, rent,汽油 |
| PPI | Producer prices (what businesses pay) | Raw materials, wholesale |
| Core CPI | CPI minus food & energy | Underlying inflation |
| PCE | Personal Consumption Expenditures | Fed's preferred measure |

**Why it matters:**
- High inflation = central banks raise rates
- Low inflation = central banks cut rates
- Inflation erodes purchasing power

**Global inflation rates (2024):**
```
US: ~3.0% (falling)
UK: ~3.0% (falling from 11% peak)
Eurozone: ~2.5% (falling)
Japan: ~2.0% (rising from zero)
```

### 4. Central Bank Policy

**What it is:** Interest rate decisions and monetary policy statements

**Key events:**

| Central Bank | Key Rate | Policy Body |
|--------------|-----------|--------------|
| US Federal Reserve | Fed Funds Rate | FOMC (8 meetings/year) |
| UK Bank of England | Bank Rate | MPC (8 meetings/year) |
| European Central Bank | Deposit Facility Rate | Governing Council (~6/year) |
| Japan Bank of Japan | Policy Rate | Policy Board |

**What to watch:**
- Rate decision (what changed?)
- Statement (tone, language changes)
- Press conference (Volcker rule: markets move on Q&A)
- Minutes (released 2-3 weeks later)

### 5. Consumer Confidence / Sentiment

**What it is:** Surveys measuring how consumers feel about the economy

**Key surveys:**
- **US:** University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment
- **UK:** GfK Consumer Confidence Index
- **Europe:** European Commission Consumer Confidence

**Why it matters:**
- Consumers = 70% of the economy
- High confidence = high spending
- Low confidence = recession risk

## 📈 Other Important Indicators

### Manufacturing & Services PMI

**What it is:** Survey of purchasing managers (businesses)

**How it works:**
- 50 = no change
- Above 50 = expansion
- Below 50 = contraction

**Key releases:**
- US: ISM Manufacturing (1st business day)
- US: ISM Services (3rd business day)
- Global: S&P Global PMIs

### Retail Sales

**What it is:** Measures consumer spending

**Why it matters:**
- Direct measure of consumer health
- Leads GDP

**US: Census Bureau (mid-month)**
**UK: ONS (monthly)**

### Housing Data

**What it is:** Home sales, starts, prices

**Key indicators:**
- **Housing Starts:** New construction
- **Existing Home Sales:** Resale market
- **Case-Shiller:** Home price index

**Why it matters:**
- Housing = large portion of economy
- Sensitive to interest rates

### Trade Balance

**What it is:** Exports minus imports

**Why it matters:**
- Shows competitiveness
- Affects currency
- Can be large (China, Germany)

## 🎯 How to Use Economic Indicators

### Step 1: Know the Schedule
Put these dates in your calendar:
- US Jobs: First Friday of month
- CPI: First Friday after month-end
- GDP: 4 weeks after quarter end
- FOMC: 8 specific dates per year

### Step 2: Know the Consensus
Before the release, know what economists expect:
- Markets already price in the consensus
- The "surprise" is what moves markets

**Real example:**
```
Consensus: CPI +0.2% month-over-month
Actual:   CPI +0.4% month-over-month
Result:   Bonds sold off, stocks wobbled, Fed hawkish
```

### Step 3: Read the Internals
Don't just look at the headline number:
- Is the details better or worse?
- Are there revisions to prior months?
- What's the trend over 3-6 months?

### Step 4: Connect to Policy
Ask: "What does this mean for central banks?"

| Data | Fed reaction |
|------|---------------|
| Strong jobs + high inflation | Hike rates |
| Weak jobs + low inflation | Cut rates |
| Strong jobs + low inflation | Hold / data dependent |

### Step 5: Don't Overreact to One Number
One data point is noise. Look for trends:
- Three months of data > one month
- The trend > the level

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Three types of indicators:**
   - Leading (predict future)
   - Coincident (show present)
   - Lagging (confirm past)

2. **The Big Five:**
   - GDP (economic output)
   - Employment (jobs report)
   - Inflation (CPI/PCE)
   - Central bank policy (rates)
   - Consumer confidence (sentiment)

3. **How to use indicators:**
   - Know the schedule
   - Know the consensus
   - Read the internals
   - Connect to policy
   - Don't overreact to one number

4. **Markets price in expectations:**
   - The surprise (actual vs. consensus) moves markets
   - Not the number itself

5. **Look for trends:**
   - One month is noise
   - Three months is a signal
