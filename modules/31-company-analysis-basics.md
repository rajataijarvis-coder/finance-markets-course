## 🤔 Why Analyze Companies?

When you buy a stock, you're buying a piece of a business. But most people don't think of it that way.

They think:
- "Stock go up → buy"
- "Stock go down → sell"
- "Everyone else is buying → buy"

But successful investors think like business analysts:
- "What does this company do?"
- "How does it make money?"
- "Is it well-managed?"
- "Is it fairly priced?"

In this module, you'll learn the basics of analyzing a company — what to look for, where to find information, and how to think about a business.

---

## 📖 The Three Questions

Every company analysis answers three questions:

### 1. What does the company do?
**The business model** — How does it make money?

### 2. How well is it doing?
**The financials** — Is it profitable? Growing? Generating cash?

### 3. Is it worth buying?
**The valuation** — Is the stock price fair?

Let's go through each.

---

## 🔍 Question 1: What Does the Company Do?

### The Business Model

Before buying a stock, you should understand:
- What product or service does the company sell?
- Who buys it?
- How do they charge? (One-time? Recurring? Subscription?)
- What's the competitive advantage?

**Real examples:**

| Company | What they do | How they make money |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Apple | Sell devices, services | Hardware sales + App Store |
| Shell | Oil & gas extraction | Selling barrels of oil |
| Unilever | Consumer goods | Selling soap, food, ice cream |
| SAP | Enterprise software | Software licenses + cloud |
| BP | Energy | Oil, gas, renewables |

### Types of Business Models

**1. Product Sales (One-time)**
- Sell physical goods
- Revenue when sale is made
- Example: Car manufacturers, retailers

**2. Subscription (Recurring)**
- Revenue over time
- Predictable cash flows
- Example: Netflix, Spotify, software SaaS

**3. Transaction-Based**
- Fee per transaction
- Volume-driven
- Example: PayPal, Visa, Amazon Marketplace

**4. Marketplace**
- Connect buyers and sellers
- Take a cut
- Example: eBay, Airbnb, Uber

**5. Advertising**
- Free product, sell attention
- Example: Google, Meta, TikTok

### Moats: The Competitive Advantage

A "moat" is something that protects a company from competitors.

**Types of moats:**

| Moat | What it is | Examples |
|------|------------|-----------|
| Brand | People pay more for the name | Apple, Louis Vuitton |
| Network effects | More users = more value | Visa, Facebook |
| Switching costs | Hard to leave | Enterprise software |
| Cost advantages | Cheaper to produce | Walmart, Amazon |
| Intangibles | Patents, licenses | Pharma, media |
| Efficiency | Better at doing something | Southwest Airlines |

> 💡 **Key insight:** A company with a strong moat is worth paying more for. A company without a moat will always face competition.

## 📊 Question 2: How Well Is It Doing?

### The Financial Statements

Three main documents tell you how a company is performing:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           INCOME STATEMENT              │
│  Revenue → Gross Profit → Net Income    │
│  (Sales)  (Profit after costs)  (Bottom)│
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
              ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│            BALANCE SHEET                │
│   Assets = Liabilities + Equity         │
│   (What owned)    (What owed) (Owner's) │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
              ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          CASH FLOW STATEMENT            │
│  Operating → Investing → Financing     │
│  (Business)  (Capex)    (Debt/Equity)  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Key Metrics to Know

#### 1. Revenue
- Total sales
- Year-over-year growth
- "Top line" (first line of income statement)

#### 2. Gross Profit & Margin
```
Gross Profit = Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold
Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue
```
- Shows how efficiently they produce
- 70% margin = very good (software)
- 20% margin = normal (retail)

#### 3. Operating Profit & Margin
```
Operating Profit = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses
Operating Margin = Operating Profit / Revenue
```
- Shows operational efficiency
- 20%+ = strong

#### 4. Net Income
```
Net Income = Revenue - All Expenses - Taxes
```
- The "bottom line"
- Profit available to shareholders

#### 5. Earnings Per Share (EPS)
```
EPS = Net Income / Shares Outstanding
```
- Company's profit per share
- Used for valuation (P/E ratio)

#### 6. Free Cash Flow (FCF)
```
FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures
```
- Cash actually generated
- More reliable than accounting profit

### Real Example: Apple (FY2023)

```
Revenue:           $383 billion
Gross Profit:      $170 billion     (44% margin)
Operating Profit:  $107 billion     (28% margin)
Net Income:        $97 billion      (25% margin)
EPS:               $6.13
Free Cash Flow:    ~$100 billion
```

This is a hugely profitable company.

## 💰 Question 3: Is It Worth Buying?

### Valuation: The Price vs. Value Game

A stock has two prices:
- **Market price** = what you pay (set by the market)
- **Intrinsic value** = what it's worth (your estimate)

**The goal:** Buy when market price < intrinsic value.

### Common Valuation Methods

#### 1. Price-to-Earnings (P/E)

```
P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share
```

- How much you pay for £1 of earnings
- Lower = "cheaper" (but can be a trap)
- Higher = "expensive" (but can be justified)

**P/E Benchmarks:**
- S&P 500 average: ~20x
- Tech stocks: 25-50x (high growth)
- Banks: 8-12x (low growth, high leverage)

**Real examples (2024):**
```
Apple:     P/E ~30x (premium for quality)
Shell:     P/E ~8x  (energy is cyclical)
Unilever:  P/E ~18x (steady consumer)
```

#### 2. Price-to-Sales (P/S)

```
P/S = Market Cap / Revenue
```

- Useful for unprofitable companies
- Lower = cheaper

#### 3. Dividend Yield

```
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend / Stock Price
```

- Income-focused measure
- Higher = more income

**UK examples:**
```
Shell:     ~4% yield
BP:        ~5% yield
Unilever:  ~3% yield
Vodafone:  ~10% yield (high yield = high risk)
```

#### 4. EV/EBITDA

```
EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA
```

- Compares enterprise value to operating profit
- Useful for comparing across leverage levels

### Growth vs. Value: Two Styles

**Growth Investing:**
- Focus on companies growing fast
- Accept higher valuations
- Example: Tech, biotech, new industries

**Value Investing:**
- Focus on companies trading below intrinsic value
- Look for "cheap" stocks
- Example: Banks, energy, utilities

> 📊 **There's no "right" answer.** Both styles can make money. Know which one fits your personality.

## 🔬 How to Analyze a Company: The Process

### Step 1: Understand the Business
- What do they sell?
- Who are their customers?
- What's the business model?
- What's their moat?

### Step 2: Look at the Numbers
- Revenue growth (last 5 years)
- Profit margins
- Free cash flow generation
- Debt levels

### Step 3: Check the Valuation
- P/E vs. historical range
- P/E vs. competitors
- Dividend yield
- Growth rate vs. price

### Step 4: Understand the Risks
- What could go wrong?
- Is the industry cyclical?
- Any regulatory risks?
- Currency risks (for global companies)?

### Step 5: Make a Decision
- Do you want to own this business?
- At what price?
- What's your thesis?

## 🌍 Where to Find Company Information

### Annual Reports
- Company's investor relations website
- SEC filings (sec.gov) for US companies
- Companies House (companieshouse.gov.uk) for UK

### Financial Data
- Bloomberg (professional)
- Yahoo Finance (free, good for basics)
- Morningstar (good for analysis)
- Google Finance (basic)

### News & Research
- Company press releases
- Earnings call transcripts (seekingalpha.com)
- Analyst reports (limited free access)
- Financial Times, Wall Street Journal

### Quantitative Screens
- Finviz (finviz.com) — free stock screener
- TradingView — charts + screening
- Yahoo Finance — basic screening

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Three questions for company analysis:**
   - What does the company do? (business model)
   - How well is it doing? (financials)
   - Is it worth buying? (valuation)

2. **Business models:**
   - Product, subscription, transaction, marketplace, advertising
   - Know what moat the company has (or doesn't)

3. **Key financial metrics:**
   - Revenue, profit margins, EPS, free cash flow
   - Understand the three statements

4. **Valuation methods:**
   - P/E, P/S, dividend yield, EV/EBITDA
   - Compare to history, competitors, growth

5. **The process:**
   - Understand → Numbers → Valuation → Risks → Decide
