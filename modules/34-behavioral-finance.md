## 🤔 Why Do Smart People Make Dumb Investment Decisions?

You've done the math. You know diversification is good. You understand P/E ratios. But when the market crashes, you panic sell. When everyone says "buy," you FOMO in. When a stock drops 20%, you can't sleep.

**Why?**

Traditional finance assumes we're rational. But we're not. We're human.

This is behavioral finance — the study of how psychology affects financial decisions.

In this module, we'll cover:
- Why we make investment mistakes
- The most common biases
- How to recognize and overcome them
- Building a system to protect yourself

---

## 📖 The Two Systems of Thinking

### System 1: Fast Thinking (Intuition)
- Automatic
- Emotional
- Instinctive
- "Buy because everyone is buying"

### System 2: Slow Thinking (Rational)
- Analytical
- Logical
- Deliberate
- "Let me check the valuation first"

> 💡 **Key insight:** Most investment decisions use System 1. But the best investors train themselves to use System 2.

## 🔴 The Big Biases in Investing

### Bias 1: Loss Aversion

**What it is:** We feel losses more intensely than gains

**The numbers:**
- Losing £100 feels worse than winning £100 feels good
- Research shows losses are 2-2.5x more painful than equivalent gains

**The impact:**
- Hold losing stocks too long ("it will come back")
- Sell winning stocks too early ("secure the gain")
- Fear prevents action

**Real example:**
```
You buy a stock at £100
It drops to £80 (you lose £20)
You hold... it drops to £70... £60... you panic sell at £60
Later: It recovers to £120

Result: You lost because you sold at the bottom
```

**The fix:**
- Define "sell" criteria in advance
- Accept that losses are part of investing
- Focus on portfolio performance, not individual stocks

### Bias 2: Confirmation Bias

**What it is:** We seek information that confirms our beliefs

**The impact:**
- Only read news sources that agree with you
- Ignore warning signs
- Don't reconsider when you should

**Real example:**
```
You believe "Tech stocks will keep going up"
You only read tech bull articles
You ignore that valuations are extreme
You don't see the crash coming
```

**The fix:**
- Seek out contrary viewpoints
- Ask: "Why might I be wrong?"
- Read sources you disagree with

### Bias 3: Herding (FOMO)

**What it is:** We follow what everyone else is doing

**The impact:**
- Buy at the top ("everyone is making money")
- Sell at the bottom ("everyone is panic selling")

**The cycle:**
```
Prices rise → More people buy → Prices rise faster
Prices fall → More people sell → Prices fall faster
```

**Real example: 2020-2022 Tech Bubble**
```
2020: Tech stocks surge
2021: Everyone "can't lose" with tech
2022: Bubble bursts, retail investors get crushed
```

**The fix:**
- Have an investment thesis independent of the crowd
- Remember: "The crowd is usually wrong at extremes"
- Don't confuse popularity with value

### Bias 4: Anchoring

**What it is:** We fixate on a specific number (usually the price we paid)

**The impact:**
- "I bought at £100, so I'll wait until £100 to sell"
- "This stock can't go below £50"
- Ignore what the company is actually worth

**Real example:**
```
You buy a stock at £100
It falls to £70
You won't sell because "I paid £100"

But: The company might now be worth £50
You're anchored to an irrelevant number
```

**The fix:**
- Don't anchor to your purchase price
- Ask: "Is this stock worth more or less than today's price?"
- Use objective valuation methods

### Bias 5: Overconfidence

**What it is:** We overestimate our abilities

**Forms:**
- "I can pick stocks better than the market"
- "I know when the top is"
- "This time is different"

**The impact:**
- Trade too much
- Take too much risk
- Ignore expert advice

**Real example:**
```
"I'm a good investor, I don't need diversification"
→ 2008: Enron employees who put 401(k) in Enron stock lost everything
```

**The fix:**
- Accept that you're probably average
- Diversify to reduce single-stock risk
- Track your performance honestly

### Bias 6: Recency Bias

**What it is:** We overweight recent events

**The impact:**
- "Stocks always go up" after a bull market
- "The market is doomed" after a crash
- Recent performance predicts nothing about future

**The math:**
```
Past 3 years: +40% returns
Next 3 years: ? (unrelated)

Past 3 years: -20% returns  
Next 3 years: ? (unrelated)
```

**The fix:**
- Look at long-term data (10+ years)
- Don't assume recent trends continue
- Remember: "What goes up must come down, and vice versa"

### Bias 7: Sunk Cost Fallacy

**What it is:** Continuing something because of what you've already invested

**The phrase:** "I've already put so much in, I can't quit now"

**The impact:**
- Holding bad investments
- Throwing good money after bad

**Real example:**
```
You invest £10,000 in a failing startup
It loses £5,000
"You should invest more to turn it around!"
→ Actually: You should cut your losses
```

**The fix:**
- Ask: "If I had £10,000 today, would I invest in this?"
- If no, sell/cut losses
- Past losses are "sunk" — irrelevant to future decisions

## 🎯 Building a System to Protect Yourself

### System 1: Pre-Commit to Rules

Before emotions hit, write down your rules:

**Example Rules:**
```
1. Rebalance quarterly
2. Never invest more than 5% in any single stock
3. If a stock drops 20%, review thesis (don't just hold or sell)
4. Wait 24 hours before making any trade
5. Review portfolio once per month, not daily
```

### System 2: Automate

- **Dollar-cost average:** Invest fixed amount monthly (removes timing decision)
- **Automatic rebalancing:** Set triggers to rebalance (removes emotional decision)
- **Direct deposit:** Set up recurring investments

### System 3: Create Friction

- **Don't check daily:** Remove portfolio from home screen
- **Longer holding period:** Minimum 1 year (reduces trading)
- **Approval waiting:** Wait 24 hours before any trade

### System 4: Accountability

- **Share goals:** Tell someone your plan
- **Hire a coach:** Financial advisor can reduce emotional decisions
- **Track performance:** Regular review keeps you honest

### System 5: The "Worst Case" Test

Before any investment, ask:
- "If this goes to zero, can I handle it?"
- "If the market drops 50%, will I sell?"
- "Do I understand what I'm buying?"

If you can't answer confidently, don't invest.

## 📊 The Investor's Emotional Cycle

The classic cycle of investor emotions:

```
     Optimism → Confidence → Euphoria → Anxiety → Denial → Despair → Disgust
            ↑                                                                     ↓
      (Top - "Can't Lose")                                          (Bottom - "Never Recover")
            ↑                                                                     ↓
      (Bull Market)                                                    (Bear Market)
```

**The pattern:**
1. Bull market starts: Optimism
2. Market rises: Confidence
3. Extended gains: Euphoria ("this time is different")
4. Market peaks: Everyone is bullish
5. Market drops: Anxiety
6. More drops: Denial ("it's a buying opportunity")
7. Further drops: Despair ("sell everything")
8. Bottom: Disgust ("I'll never invest again")
9. New bull: Optimism (cycle repeats)

> 💡 **The key:** Recognize which phase you're in. Be fearful when others are greedy, greedy when others are fearful.

## 🌎 Famous Behavioral Mistakes

### The Nasdaq Bubble (2000)

**What happened:** Tech stocks surged, everyone believed "the new economy" had no limits.

**The mistake:** Ignoring valuations, herding, overconfidence

**The result:** 78% drop in Nasdaq

### The 2008 Crisis

**What happened:** Housing prices always go up... right?

**The mistake:** Overconfidence, anchoring, ignoring risk

**The result:** -50% in global stocks

### The GameStop Short Squeeze (2021)

**What happened:** Reddit vs. hedge funds

**The mistake:** Herding, FOMO, ignoring fundamentals

**The result:** Massive volatility, many retail investors lost money

### The 2022 Bear Market

**What happened:** "Stocks only go up" after decades of low rates

**The mistake:** Recency bias, underestimating inflation

**The result:** -25% S&P 500, -37% Nasdaq

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Two systems:**
   - System 1: Fast, emotional, intuitive
   - System 2: Slow, rational, analytical

2. **Big biases:**
   - Loss aversion: Fear of losses > desire for gains
   - Confirmation bias: Seeking evidence you agree with
   - Herding: Following the crowd
   - Anchoring: Fixating on irrelevant numbers
   - Overconfidence: Thinking you're better than average
   - Recency: Overweighting recent events
   - Sunk cost: Throwing good money after bad

3. **Protect yourself:**
   - Pre-commit to rules
   - Automate decisions
   - Create friction
   - Use accountability
   - Test worst-case scenarios

4. **The emotional cycle:**
   - Recognize which phase you're in
   - Be contrarian at extremes

5. **The goal:** Make System 2 decisions, not System 1 reactions
