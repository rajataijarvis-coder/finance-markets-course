## 🤔 The Big Question

You've invested in the stock market. Your shares in Apple (AAPL) grew from $15,000 to $25,000. That's a $10,000 profit.

But here's the question: **Do you pay tax on that profit?**

The answer depends on where you live, how long you held the investment, and what kind of account you used. Welcome to the world of **capital gains tax (CGT)**.

In this module, we'll cover:
- What capital gains tax actually is
- How it works in the UK, US, and Europe
- Key exemptions and strategies
- Real examples with global stocks

---

## 📖 What is Capital Gains Tax?

### The Basic Concept

**Capital gains tax** is a tax on the profit you make when you sell an asset for more than you paid for it.

```
Profit = Sale Price − Purchase Price − Costs

Tax = Profit × Capital Gains Tax Rate
```

**Example:**
```
You bought 100 shares of Tesla (TSLA) at $200/share = $20,000
You sold 100 shares at $250/share = $25,000
Profit = $5,000
If tax rate = 20%, you owe $1,000 in tax
```

### What Assets Are Taxed?

Different countries tax different things. Generally:

| Asset Type | UK | US | Germany |
|------------|----|----|---------|
| Stocks & Shares | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Property (not your home) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Crypto | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Art & Collectibles | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Foreign currency | ❌ | ✅ (specific rules) | ❌ |

## 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 UK: Capital Gains Tax

### The UK System

The UK taxes gains on **disposals** of assets. A disposal happens when you:
- Sell an asset
- Give it away (even to family)
- Exchange it for something else

**Key UK Facts:**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Annual Exemption | £3,000 (2024/25) |
| Basic Rate Tax | 18% or 20% (depending on income) |
| Higher Rate Tax | 20% or 24% (depending on income) |
| Property (non-residential) | 20% (no annual exemption) |

### UK Tax Rates Explained

The rate depends on your **total taxable income**.

```
Example: UK Investor
Income: £25,000/year
Stocks profit: £8,000

Your total income pushes you into Basic Rate band
CGT rate on stocks: 20%
Tax owed: £8,000 × 20% = £1,600
```

**But wait — the annual exemption!**

```
Example with exemption:
Stocks profit: £8,000
Minus annual exemption: £3,000
Taxable gain: £5,000
Tax owed: £5,000 × 20% = £1,000

You saved £600!
```

## 🇺🇸 US: Capital Gains Tax

### The US System

The US has **two types** of capital gains:

1. **Short-term capital gains** — assets held for **one year or less**
2. **Long-term capital gains** — assets held for **more than one year**

**Why the difference?** The government wants to encourage long-term investing. Long-term gains get preferential treatment.

### US Tax Rates (2024)

**Short-term gains** are taxed at your **ordinary income tax rate** (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37%).

**Long-term gains** use special rates:

| Taxable Income (Single) | Long-Term Rate |
|------------------------|----------------|
| $0 – $47,025 | 0% |
| $47,026 – $518,900 | 15% |
| $518,901+ | 20% |

| Taxable Income (Married Filing Jointly) | Long-Term Rate |
|----------------------------------------|----------------|
| $0 – $94,050 | 0% |
| $94,051 – $583,750 | 15% |
| $583,751+ | 20% |

**Plus:** Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) of 3.8% applies if your income is above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married).

### US Example

```
Example: US Investor
Income: $80,000/year (single)
Invested in: 50 shares of BP (British Petroleum) at £30 = $2,100
Sold for: £50 = $3,500
Profit: $1,400
Held for: 18 months (long-term)

Long-term rate: 15%
Tax owed: $1,400 × 15% = $210
```

**Not bad!** Compare to ordinary income at 22%: $1,400 × 22% = $308.

**You saved $98 by holding for more than a year.**

## 🇪🇺 Europe: A Patchwork of Rules

European countries each have their own systems. Let's look at a few key ones:

### Germany

- **No explicit CGT** on stocks held for more than one year
- Gains are taxed as **income** if held less than one year
- **Solidarity surcharge** of 5.5% on top
- Church tax may apply (8-9%)

```
Example: German Investor
Bought: SAP shares at €10,000
Sold: €14,000 after 8 months
Profit: €4,000
Taxed as income at ~25-45%
```

### France

- **Flat rate** of 30% (12.8% tax + 17.2% social charges)
- But **teasing exception:** Gains on French-listed shares held >2 years get a **rebate**
- After 2 years: 50% of gains exempt
- After 3 years: 65% of gains exempt

```
Example: French Investor
Bought: LVMH shares at €5,000
Sold: €7,000 after 3 years
Profit: €2,000
65% exempt → Taxable gain: €700
Tax: €700 × 30% = €210
```

### Netherlands

- **No CGT** on assets (weird but true!)
- Instead: 36.97% tax on **deemed return** (not actual gains)
- Based on a fictional 6% return, regardless of actual performance

## 💡 Key Strategies

### 1. Hold Long-Term

The simplest strategy: **don't sell**.

```
UK: 10%/20% (long-term) vs 18%/20% (short-term)
US: 0%/15%/20% (long-term) vs 10-37% (short-term)
```

The difference can be thousands of pounds/dollars in tax savings.

### 2. Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts

| Country | Account | Benefit |
|---------|---------|---------|
| UK | ISA | All gains tax-free |
| UK | SIPP | All gains tax-free |
| US | 401(k) | Tax-deferred |
| US | IRA | Tax-free growth (Roth) or tax deduction (Traditional) |
| Germany | Depot | Tax-free if held >1 year |
| Netherlands | Box 3 | No CGT, but deemed return |

### 3. Harvest Losses

**Tax-loss harvesting** = selling losing positions to offset gains.

```
Example:
Gain on AAPL: £5,000
Loss on TSLA: £3,000
Net gain: £2,000
Tax on £2,000 at 20% = £400

vs. if you didn't harvest:
Tax on £5,000 at 20% = £1,000

You saved £600!
```

**Important:** Watch out for the **wash sale rule** (US) or similar rules (UK) — don't buy the same or "substantially identical" asset within 30 days.

### 4. Offset Against Capital Losses

You can use **capital losses** to reduce your **capital gains**.

- Gains and losses in the same tax year can be netted
- If losses exceed gains, you can carry forward to future years
- In the UK: Losses can be carried forward indefinitely

## 📊 Real-World Examples

### Example 1: UK Investor with FTSE 100

```
Scenario:
- UK basic rate taxpayer
- Bought 500 shares of Unilever (ULVR) at £35
- Sold at £45 after 18 months
- Profit: (£45 - £35) × 500 = £5,000

Calculation:
- Annual exemption: £3,000
- Taxable gain: £2,000
- CGT at 20%: £400

Effective tax rate: 400/5000 = 8%
```

### Example 2: US Investor with S&P 500

```
Scenario:
- US investor, income $60,000 (single)
- Bought 100 shares of index fund (SPY) at $400
- Sold at $500 after 2 years
- Profit: ($500 - $400) × 100 = $10,000

Calculation:
- Long-term rate: 15%
- Tax owed: $10,000 × 15% = $1,500

Effective tax rate: 15%
```

### Example 3: European Investor with Euro Stocks

```
Scenario:
- German investor
- Bought 200 shares of Siemens at €100
- Sold at €130 after 3 years
- Profit: €6,000

Calculation:
- Held >1 year = no CGT
- But: church tax may apply (~€540 if religious)
```

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

### ❌ Mistake 1: Forgetting Annual Exemptions

In the UK, you have £3,000 exemption. Use it or lose it!

### ❌ Mistake 2: Wash Sales

In the US, if you sell at a loss and buy back within 30 days, you can't claim the loss.

### ❌ Mistake 3: Not Reporting

Even if you don't owe tax (because of exemptions or losses), you may still need to report.

### ❌ Mistake 4: Assuming All Countries Same Rules

Your UK-domiciled friend might not owe tax on a gain, but as a US citizen, you might.

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Capital gains tax** is a tax on profit from selling assets for more than you paid
2. **UK** has £3,000 annual exemption; rates are 10%/20% (long-term) or 18%/20% (short-term)
3. **US** taxes short-term gains as income; long-term gains get 0%/15%/20% rates
4. **Europe** varies wildly — Germany taxes income rates on short-term, nothing on long-term
5. **Strategy #1:** Hold investments for more than a year
6. **Strategy #2:** Use tax-advantaged accounts (ISA, 401k, IRA)
7. **Strategy #3:** Harvest losses to offset gains
