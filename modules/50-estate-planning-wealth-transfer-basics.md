## 🤔 The Big Question

You've built wealth — stocks, pensions, property, savings. But what happens to all of it when you're gone?

**Estate planning** is how you control what happens to your wealth after you die. Without a plan, the state decides — and taxes might eat huge chunks of what you've built.

In this module, we'll cover:
- What estate planning actually is
- UK inheritance tax
- US estate and gift tax
- European succession rules
- Key documents (wills, trusts)
- Practical strategies

## 📖 What is Estate Planning?

### The Basic Concept

**Estate** = everything you own
**Estate planning** = deciding who gets what, when, and how

```
Your Estate
    │
    ├── Assets: cash, investments, property, possessions
    │
    └── Debts: mortgages, loans, taxes owed

Estate Planning Controls:
    │
    ├── Who inherits what
    │
    ├── How much tax is paid (or avoided)
    │
    └── Who manages affairs if you're incapacitated
```

### Why It Matters

```
Without a Will (UK):
- Assets distributed by "intestacy rules"
- Spouse/civil partner gets priority
- No control over exactly who gets what

Without Estate Planning (US):
- Estate tax can take 40%+ of assets
- Families can lose homes, businesses, savings

With Good Planning:
- Assets go where YOU want
- Taxes minimized
- Family protected
```

## 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 UK: Inheritance Tax

### UK Inheritance Tax (IHT)

**What it is:** A tax on the estate of someone who has died.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Rate | 40% (above threshold) |
| Threshold (2024/25) | £325,000 |
| Residence nil-rate band | £175,000 (if property left to children/descendants) |
| exemptions | Spouse/civil partner transfers (unlimited) |
| Exemptions | Gifts to charity (100% exempt) |
| Annual gift allowance | £3,000/year |

### UK IHT Example

```
Scenario: Margaret's estate
- Property: £500,000
- Stocks & ISA: £300,000
- Cash: £50,000
- Other assets: £50,000
- Total: £900,000

Calculation:
- Threshold: £325,000
- Residence nil-rate band: £175,000
- Total allowances: £500,000

Taxable: £900,000 - £500,000 = £400,000
Tax at 40%: £400,000 × 40% = £160,000

Net to family: £740,000
```

### UK Exemptions (Important!)

```
GIFTS THAT ARE IHT-FREE:

✓ Spouse/civil partner transfers
✓ Charity donations
✓ Gifts to political parties
✓ Gifts to museums, libraries
✓ Annual allowance: £3,000
✓ Small gifts: £250 per person/year
✓ Wedding gifts: £5,000 (child), £2,500 (grandchild)
✓ Regular gifts from income (after tax year)
```

### The 7-Year Rule

**Gifts made more than 7 years before death are IHT-free.**

```
Example: David gives £200,000 to his children
- Year 0: Gift of £200,000
- Year 5: David dies

Taper relief (3-7 years):
- After 5 years: 32% reduced (from 40%)
- Tax on gift: £200,000 × 8% = £16,000

Without the gift:
- Would be taxed at 40% = £80,000
- Saved: £64,000!
```

## 🇺🇸 US: Estate and Gift Tax

### US Estate Tax

**What it is:** Federal tax on assets transferred at death.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Federal exemption (2024) | $13.61 million (per person) |
| Federal rate | Up to 40% (above exemption) |
| State estate taxes | CA, CT, HI, IL, MA, ME, MD, MN, NY, OR, RI, VT, WA |
| State exemption | Varies (often much lower than federal) |
| Portability | Yes — spouse can inherit exemption |

### US Gift Tax

**What it is:** Tax on gifts given during lifetime.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Annual exclusion | $18,000 (2024) |
| Lifetime exemption | Same as estate tax ($13.61M) |
| Tax rate | Up to 40% (above lifetime exemption) |

### US Example

```
Scenario: John's estate (California)
- Home: $2,000,000
- Stocks: $8,000,000
- Other assets: $1,000,000
- Total: $11,000,000

Calculation:
- Federal exemption: $13.61M
- California: No state estate tax!

Federal tax: $0 (under exemption)
Net to family: $11,000,000

If over exemption:
- First $13.61M: $0
- Excess at 40%
```

### Portability (US)

**Spouses can combine exemptions:**

```
Example: Married couple
- Husband's exemption: $13.61M
- Wife's exemption: $13.61M
- Combined: $27.22M

Portability: Remaining exemption transfers to surviving spouse
(No need for complex trust structures!)
```

## 🇪🇺 Europe: Succession Rules

### EU Succession Regulation

Since 2015, EU countries follow **Regulation (EU) No 650/2012**:

- Your "habitual residence" determines which country's law applies
- You can choose the law of your nationality
- Applies to assets in EU countries

### Key Country Rules

| Country | Key Feature |
|---------|-------------|
| **Germany** | Forced heirship: children get 50% regardless of will |
| **France** | Forced heirship: children get 75%+ |
| **Spain** | Forced heirship: children get 50% (one-third reserved, one-third "mejora") |
| **Netherlands** | More flexible — can leave to anyone |
| **Italy** | Forced heirship: children get 50% |

### UK Rules

- **No forced heirship** — you can leave assets to anyone
- **IHT applies** — 40% above threshold
- **Wills are respected** — subject to IHT

## 💡 Key Documents

### 1. Will

**What it is:** Legal document stating who gets your assets.

```
WHY IT MATTERS:
├── Without a will: State decides (intestacy)
├── With a will: You decide
├── Can name guardians for children
└── Can minimize taxes
```

### 2. Trust

**What it is:** Legal arrangement where someone holds assets for beneficiaries.

```
TYPES:

1. Revocable Living Trust
   ├── You control during lifetime
   ├── Avoids probate at death
   └── Can be changed anytime

2. Irrevocable Trust
   ├── Cannot be changed once set up
   ├── Removes assets from estate (IHT protection)
   └── Used for tax planning

3. Discretionary Trust
   ├── Trustees decide distributions
   ├── Flexibility for beneficiaries
   └── More complex tax rules
```

### 3. Power of Attorney

**What it is:** Document naming someone to act on your behalf if you're incapacitated.

```
TYPES:

1. Property & Financial Affairs
   └── Manage money, pay bills

2. Health & Welfare
   └── Make medical decisions
```

### 4. Letter of Wishes

**What it is:** Non-binding note explaining your wishes (especially for trusts).

```
Use for:
- Explaining why you made certain bequests
- Guiding trustees on distributions
- Expressing values and hopes
```

## 📊 Real-World Examples

### Example 1: UK Family Home

```
Scenario: Robert and Sarah, UK
- Family home: £650,000
- Stocks: £200,000
- ISAs: £150,000
- Pension: £300,000
- Total: £1,300,000

Plan:
├── Leave home to spouse (IHT-free transfer)
├── Use ISAs for children (within thresholds)
└── Pension usually outside IHT

IHT calculation after first spouse death:
- Home to spouse: £0 IHT
- Other assets pass: IHT-free to spouse
- Both die: Children inherit

Potential savings: £160,000+ in IHT
```

### Example 2: US Small Business Owner

```
Scenario: Maria, US
- Business: $3,000,000 (lifetime lease)
- Home: $800,000
- Stocks: $1,200,000
- Total: $5,000,000

Plan:
├── Business in LLC (discounted value)
├── Main residence in trust
├── Annual gifting to children ($18,000/year each)
└── Lifetime exemption: $13.61M

IHT exposure: $0
Business continues smoothly
```

### Example 3: European Property

```
Scenario: Hans (German) owns French property
- German law applies (habitual residence)
- French property: €500,000
- German home: €400,000
- Total: €900,000

German forced heirship:
- Children get 50% = €450,000
- Hans can direct remaining 50%

French property under German law:
- Children have claim even on French asset

Planning:
- Consider changing habitual residence
- Use succession planning early
- Gift property during lifetime
```

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

### ❌ Mistake 1: No Will

Dying without a will = state decides. Not what you want.

### ❌ Mistake 2: Forgetting Joint Assets

Jointly held assets pass automatically to survivor — may not match your wishes.

### ❌ Mistake 3: Not Updating After Life Events

Marriage, divorce, births, deaths — update your will!

### ❌ Mistake 4: Ignoring Potentially Exempt Transfers

Gifts within 7 years may still have IHT implications.

### ❌ Mistake 5: Not Considering State Taxes (US)

Federal exemption is high, but state taxes can bite (NY, CA, etc.).

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. **Estate planning** controls what happens to your wealth after death
2. **UK IHT:** 40% above £325,000 (+ £175,000 residence nil-rate band)
3. **US Estate Tax:** 40% above $13.61M federal exemption
4. **European forced heirship:** Germany, France, Spain require certain family shares
5. **Key documents:** Will, trust, power of attorney
6. **7-year rule (UK):** Gifts over 7 years before death are IHT-free
7. **Annual gifting:** Use £3,000 (UK) / $18,000 (US) annual allowances
8. **Review regularly:** After major life events, update your plan

## ✅ Quick Check

Test your understanding:

1. **UK Question:** Margaret wants to give her daughter £300,000 for a house. She's 70, in good health. What are the IHT implications if she dies in 5 years?

2. **US Question:** Tom wants to give his son $100,000 for a business. What's the most tax-efficient way to do this?

3. **Strategy Question:** You have a £1.2 million estate in the UK (mostly home). What's one strategy to reduce potential IHT?

---

## Answers

1. **UK Answer:** 
   - Gift exceeds annual allowances
   - If she dies within 7 years, IHT may be due
   - After 5 years: taper relief at 32% (40% - 8% reduction per year from years 3-7)
   - Tax: £300,000 × 8% (year 5) = £24,000 potentially due from daughter

2. **US Answer:** 
   - Use annual exclusion: $18,000/year for 5+ years
   - Or use lifetime exemption: $13.61M total
   - Best: Gift over multiple years using annual exclusion
   - $100,000 could be given as 6 annual gifts of $16,500 ($99,000) + $1,000 (within lifetime)

3. **Strategy Answer:** 
   - Leave property to spouse (unlimited IHT-free transfer)
   - Use residence nil-rate band (£175,000) 
   - Make gifts to use annual £3,000 allowance
   - Consider downsizing to release equity
   - Put assets in trust (irrevocable removes from estate)
